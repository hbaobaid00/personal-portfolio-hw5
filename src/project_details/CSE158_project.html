Findings & Visual Supporting Evidence

Through this project, I explored how musical attributes embedded in popular songs relate to trends in U.S. economic sentiment ‚Äî specifically unemployment movements. Using regression analysis, I examined whether emotions and production choices in mainstream music may reflect broader cultural or economic moods.

The regression output below highlights several meaningful behavioral relationships. Musical features such as energy and danceability were positively associated with higher unemployment, suggesting that upbeat, emotionally intense songs tend to surface during periods of economic stress. Conversely, softer traits like valence (happiness) showed a negative relationship, hinting that darker cultural tones may accompany difficult periods.

To contextualize these patterns, I examined how missing data and song representation changed over time. The temporal histogram below captures the distribution of unavailable Spotify attributes by release year, showing spikes in certain decades. This reveals both data availability issues and how the representation of music has evolved alongside cultural shifts.

Finally, a high-level dataset snapshot illustrates how average song features shifted from 1960-2021 ‚Äî including rising tempos, evolving instrumental patterns, and decreasing acoustic presence. Together, these trends help explain why music increasingly reflects energetic but emotionally complex tones.

Collectively, this work strengthened my skills in statistical inference, exploratory analysis, and narrative-based data storytelling ‚Äî while deepening my appreciation for music as a cultural barometer.
